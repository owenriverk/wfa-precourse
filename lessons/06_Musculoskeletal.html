Lesson 06: Musculoskeletal Injuries

⏱️ Estimated Reading Time: 35 minutes 📚 Wilderness First Aid Course

Overview

Learning Objectives

  • Understand skeletal anatomy and classify fracture and dislocation types
  • Perform proper neurovascular assessment (the 6 Ps and CSM)
  • Apply splinting principles and identify specific splinting techniques
  • Recognize when reduction is appropriate and when to avoid it
  • Manage open fractures, dislocations, and special scenarios (amputations, compartment syndrome)
  • Understand Ottawa Rules for ankle and knee injuries

Musculoskeletal injuries are among the most common injuries encountered in wilderness settings. A twisted ankle on a hiking trail, a fall onto an outstretched hand, or a climbing accident can all result in fractures or dislocations. In remote settings, proper field management is critical because evacuation may take hours or days.

This lesson covers the assessment and management of fractures and dislocations using wilderness-appropriate techniques. Your priorities are: assess neurovascular status, prevent further injury, immobilize/splint, reassess, and evacuate appropriately.

Critical Principle: A neurovascular compromise (absent pulse, severe pain, paralysis distal to injury) is an emergency requiring reduction or rapid evacuation. Do not delay if blood flow is compromised.

Anatomy & Injury Types

Skeletal Anatomy Basics

The skeletal system consists of:

  • Long Bones: Femur, tibia, humerus, radius, ulna. Composed of a shaft (diaphysis) and ends (epiphysis) with a growth plate in children.
  • Joints: Where two or more bones meet. Held together by ligaments. Cartilage covers bone ends to reduce friction.
  • Tendons: Connect muscles to bone.
  • Ligaments: Connect bone to bone, stabilizing joints.

Fracture Classification: Open vs. Closed

Closed Fracture: Bone is broken but skin is intact. No direct communication between fracture and environment.

Open (Compound) Fracture: Bone is broken AND skin is broken, allowing direct contamination. Risk of infection and greater blood loss. More serious than closed fractures.

Fracture Types by Pattern

Type Description Typical Mechanism
Transverse Fracture line perpendicular to bone shaft Direct blow or bending force
Oblique Fracture line at an angle (45°) to bone shaft Twisting or combined bending/axial load
Spiral Fracture line wraps around bone shaft Twisting force (common in skiing injuries)
Comminuted Bone broken into 3+ pieces High-energy trauma (e.g., car crash, fall from height)
Greenstick Incomplete break; bone bends but doesn't completely fracture (common in children) Bending force in flexible pediatric bone

Dislocation

A dislocation (luxation) is displacement of a bone from its normal position at a joint. Often accompanied by tearing of ligaments and supporting structures. Common sites: shoulder, knee, ankle, finger.

Sprain vs. Strain

  • Sprain: Tear of ligament (bone-to-bone) from excessive joint motion. Usually swelling, bruising, pain with movement.
  • Strain: Tear of muscle or tendon. May be acute or chronic. Pain and weakness.

Mechanism of Injury Patterns

Understanding the mechanism helps predict which structures are injured:

  • Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH): Often causes wrist fractures (Colles' fracture) or shoulder dislocation
  • Axial load (impact from above): Vertebral compression fractures or femur fractures
  • Twisting: Ankle sprains, spiral fractures of tibia
  • Hyperextension: Knee ligament injuries, elbow dislocations

Assessment of Musculoskeletal Injuries

Primary Assessment: DCAP-BTLS

At the injury site, assess for:

  • Deformity: Is the limb angulated or shortened compared to the opposite side?
  • Contusions: Bruising indicating trauma
  • Abrasions: Scraping suggesting mechanism
  • Punctures/lacerations: Open wounds; if over a fracture site = open fracture
  • Burns: Check for thermal injury
  • Tenderness: Palpate gently; point tenderness over bone suggests fracture
  • Lacerations: As above
  • Swelling: Edema from bleeding into tissues

Neurovascular Assessment: The 6 Ps

Critical: Perform neurovascular checks BEFORE and AFTER splinting. If neurovascular status worsens after splinting, loosen the splint immediately.

The 6 Ps of Neurovascular Assessment:

P Assessment Concerning Finding
Pain Ask about pain severity and character. Severe pain disproportionate to injury suggests compartment syndrome. Severe pain with passive stretch; pain out of proportion
Pallor Check skin color distal to injury. Compare to opposite limb. Pale, white, or cyanotic (blue) skin distal to injury
Pulse Palpate distal pulse (e.g., dorsalis pedis for ankle, radial for wrist). Absent or very weak pulse distally
Paresthesia Ask about tingling or "pins and needles" sensation. Numbness, tingling, or burning distal to injury
Paralysis Test motor function distal to injury (move fingers/toes on command). Weakness or inability to move distal structures
Pressure Palpate the limb. Look for tense, swollen compartments. Tight, swollen compartments; tenseness with passive stretch pain

CSM Assessment

CSM = Circulation, Sensation, Motion — a simpler version of the 6 Ps:

  • C - Circulation: Distal pulse, skin color/temperature, capillary refill
  • S - Sensation: Can patient feel touch/pain distal to injury?
  • M - Motion: Can patient move fingers/toes on command?

Ottawa Ankle and Knee Rules

Ottawa Ankle Rules: Imaging (X-ray) is recommended if there is tenderness over:
  • Posterior edge of lateral malleolus (outer ankle bone)
  • Posterior edge of medial malleolus (inner ankle bone)
  • Navicular bone (midfoot)
OR inability to bear weight immediately after injury and at exam.
Ottawa Knee Rules: Imaging is recommended if there is:
  • Tenderness over the patella
  • Tenderness over the fibula head or epicondyle
  • Inability to bend knee to 90° OR inability to walk/bear weight
In wilderness settings, use these rules to guide evacuation priority.

Splinting Principles & Techniques

General Splinting Principles

Golden Rules of Splinting:
  • Immobilize joints ABOVE and BELOW the fracture site
  • Pad bony prominences to prevent pressure ulcers
  • Reassess neurovascular status (6 Ps or CSM) after splinting
  • Splint in position of comfort (unless severe angulation compromises circulation)
  • Apply ice/cold if available to reduce swelling
  • Elevate if possible to reduce swelling

Common Splinting Techniques

SAM Splint (Soft Aluminum Malleable): Versatile, compact, reusable. Molds to any shape. Excellent for forearm, wrist, ankle, knee. Pad with extra material and secure with elastic bandages.

Improvised Splints: In wilderness, use materials at hand:

  • Sticks/branches padded with clothing
  • Rolled-up sleeping bag or pad alongside the limb
  • Buddy taping (taping injured finger to adjacent healthy finger)
  • Sling and swathe for upper extremity

Traction Splint (for Femur Fractures): A traction splint is the ONLY splint specifically indicated for mid-shaft femur (thighbone) fractures. It reduces pain and bleeding by restoring limb length and alignment. Examples: Kendrick traction device, Thomas splint (in hospital settings). In wilderness, apply gentle longitudinal traction while supporting the limb.

When to Reduce vs. Not Reduce

NEVER attempt reduction of:
  • Fracture-dislocations (bone fractured AND dislocated)
  • Any fracture with significant soft tissue injury
  • Shoulder dislocations in patients with severe pain or if you lack training

Consider reduction ONLY if:

  • Trained and experienced
  • Neurovascular compromise (absent pulse, paralysis, severe pain) requires improving circulation
  • Prolonged evacuation (>2-4 hours) and simple dislocation (e.g., anterior shoulder)

Dislocations

Anterior Shoulder Dislocation (Most Common)

The humeral head dislocates anteriorly out of the glenoid cavity. Classic presentation:

  • Arm held in slight abduction and external rotation ("arm held away from body")
  • Arm cannot be internally rotated
  • Severe pain
  • Palpable bulge anteriorly
  • Loss of normal rounded shoulder contour

Field Management: Immobilize in position of comfort with sling and swathe. In wilderness with prolonged evacuation, if trained, reduction may be attempted. After reduction, reassess pulse, sensation, and motor function.

Patella (Kneecap) Dislocation

Usually dislocates laterally (outward). Presents with:

  • Severe pain and quadriceps muscle spasm
  • Visible lateral displacement of patella
  • Inability to straighten knee

Field Management: Many patella dislocations reduce spontaneously with gentle knee extension. If still dislocated, gentle straightening often reduces it. Splint knee in full extension (using rolled sleeping bag/pad as backrest). Evacuate.

Finger/Toe Dislocations

Usually obvious deformity. Often reducible in field by gentle traction-and-reduction maneuver (pulling distal bone back into alignment). Once reduced, buddy tape to adjacent finger/toe. Evacuate if significant soft tissue injury or if reduction is not possible.

Hip Dislocation

Rare but serious. Most commonly posterior dislocations. Presents with:

  • Severe pain in hip/groin
  • Leg internally rotated and adducted ("fetal position")
  • Unable to move leg
  • Potential sciatic nerve injury (foot drop, sensory loss)

Field Management: Immobilize in position found. This is a neurovascular emergency — risk of avascular necrosis of femoral head if not reduced promptly. EMERGENT evacuation required.

Reduction Indications & Cautions

Indications for Reduction in Field:
  • Neurovascular compromise (absent pulse, paralysis, severe pain with passive stretch)
  • Prolonged evacuation (>4 hours estimated)
  • Responder has training and experience
  • No fracture-dislocation (i.e., bone isn't fractured at the joint)

Attempt reduction only if the above criteria are met. Otherwise, immobilize and evacuate.

Open Fractures & Special Considerations

Open Fractures: Immediate Management

Open fractures are surgical emergencies due to high infection and complication risk.

Immediate Steps:
  1. NEVER reduce an open fracture in the field. Splint in position found.
  2. Gently irrigate the wound with clean water or saline to remove dirt. Gentle irrigation is protective against infection.
  3. Cover with clean (preferably sterile) dressing. May need to dress around exposed bone.
  4. Splint and elevate to reduce swelling
  5. EMERGENT evacuation to hospital for surgical management (cleaning, internal fixation)

Amputated Parts

If a limb or digit is amputated:

  • Control bleeding on the amputated stump with direct pressure
  • Retrieve the amputated part if possible
  • Wrap it in clean, moist cloth (use clean water, saline, or even dilute bleach solution if available)
  • Place in a plastic bag with ice (keep COOL but do NOT freeze directly against ice)
  • Label with time of amputation
  • Transport with patient to hospital — reattachment may be possible if done within 6-12 hours (cold ischemia time)
  • EMERGENT evacuation

Compartment Syndrome

Definition: Increased pressure within a closed muscle compartment (group of muscles enclosed by fascia), compromising blood flow and causing tissue death if not relieved.

Classic Presentation:

  • Pain DISPROPORTIONATE to injury (hallmark finding)
  • Pain with passive stretch of muscles in the compartment (e.g., passive extension of fingers if forearm compartment involved)
  • Tight, swollen compartment on palpation
  • Paresthesia and weakness (late)

Common Sites: Anterior compartment of leg (tibia/fibula fracture), forearm, thigh.

Field Management:

  • Recognize the clinical suspicion based on disproportionate pain
  • Remove any tight dressings or splints (may worsen compartment pressure)
  • Elevate limb only to heart level (elevation beyond this reduces compartment perfusion)
  • EMERGENT evacuation to hospital for fasciotomy (surgical incision to relieve pressure)
Remember: Compartment syndrome is not obvious on X-ray or clinical exam initially. Suspicion is based on clinical findings (pain out of proportion, pain with passive stretch). Do not miss it — it requires emergency surgical decompression.

Clinical Scenarios

Scenario 1: Ankle Fracture with Intact Distal Pulses
A 26-year-old backpacker twisted her ankle stepping off a rock. She reports immediate severe pain and swelling. Exam shows obvious swelling and deformity (foot in inverted position). You assess: Dorsalis pedis pulse present and strong, foot is pink and warm, she can move her toes and feels touch. By Ottawa Ankle Rules, she has tenderness over the lateral malleolus.
Analysis: This is likely an ankle fracture with intact neurovascular status. Ottawa Ankle Rules indicate imaging (X-ray) is appropriate, but this is wilderness medicine — you may not have X-ray capability.

Field Management:
  • Treat as a fracture (assume positive until proven otherwise)
  • SAM splint the ankle, immobilizing the ankle joint. Pad bony prominences.
  • Recheck distal pulses, sensation, and motor function after splinting
  • Elevate and apply cold (snow/cold water) to reduce swelling
  • Pain control: acetaminophen or ibuprofen if available
  • Evacuation: URGENT (same day if possible) for imaging and evaluation
  • If neurovascular compromise develops (pulse disappears, foot becomes pale or cold), adjust splint immediately and prepare for emergency descent
Expected Outcome: With proper splinting and evacuation, good prognosis for recovery.
Scenario 2: Femur Fracture with Neurovascular Compromise
A rock climber falls 15 feet and lands on his leg. He has severe pain in the thigh, obvious deformity (shortened, angulated leg), and significant swelling. Distal exam shows: foot is pale and cool, dorsalis pedis pulse is absent, he cannot move toes (paralysis), severe pain. He's in shock (HR 124, RR 24, BP 96/62).
Analysis: This is a femur fracture with neurovascular compromise (absent pulse, paralysis, pale cool foot). This is an EMERGENCY. The limb will lose viability without prompt restoration of blood flow.

Immediate Actions:
  • Position patient supine with legs elevated (except the injured leg — keep at heart level)
  • Attempt gentle realignment: apply gentle traction along the length of the femur and rotate internally if the foot remains externally rotated and angulated. Goal: restore more normal alignment and attempt to restore circulation.
  • If after gentle realignment the pulse returns and foot becomes warm and pink — success, the reduction restored blood flow. Splint and stabilize.
  • If pulse remains absent — continue gentle attempts or prepare for immediate evacuation. The limb is at risk.
  • Apply traction splint (if trained and available) to stabilize and reduce pain.
  • Keep warm (patient is in shock)
  • Establish IV access if trained (treat shock)
  • EMERGENT evacuation by helicopter if available. This patient requires hospital surgery urgently.
  • Monitor vitals and neurovascular status continuously.
Key Point: Neurovascular compromise is the exception where reduction in the field is justified and life-saving.

Summary & Key Takeaways

Key Concepts to Remember:
  • Always assess neurovascular status BEFORE and AFTER splinting
  • Splint immobilizing joints above and below the fracture
  • Open fractures: irrigate gently, cover, splint in position, EMERGENT evacuation
  • Compartment syndrome: suspect if pain is out of proportion — requires emergency fasciotomy
  • Neurovascular compromise: consider gentle reduction/realignment to restore circulation
  • Ottawa Rules guide imaging decisions (ankle, knee)
  • Traction splints for femur fractures only

Glossary of Terms

Angulation
Abnormal bending or deviation of a bone from its normal axis; indicates fracture or dislocation.
Avascular Necrosis
Death of bone tissue due to loss of blood supply; can occur with hip dislocations if not reduced promptly.
Closed Fracture
A fracture where the bone is broken but the skin is intact; no communication with external environment.
Compartment Syndrome
Increased pressure within a closed muscle compartment leading to tissue hypoperfusion and necrosis; requires emergency surgical decompression.
CSM
Circulation, Sensation, Motion — simplified neurovascular assessment to evaluate distal limb status.
Dislocation (Luxation)
Complete displacement of a bone from its normal position at a joint.
FOOSH
Fall On Outstretched Hand — common mechanism causing wrist and shoulder injuries.
Fracture
A break in the continuity of bone due to trauma or pathology.
Open Fracture
A fracture where the bone is broken AND there is a break in the overlying skin; high risk for infection and blood loss.
Ottawa Ankle/Knee Rules
Evidence-based clinical decision tools to determine which patients require X-ray imaging for ankle and knee injuries.
Pulse
The regular beating of the arteries; assessed distal to injury to ensure adequate blood flow. Absent distal pulse = neurovascular emergency.
Reduction
Restoration of a dislocated bone or joint to its normal position; done to restore alignment and neurovascular supply.
Splint
A device or technique used to immobilize a fracture or dislocation, preventing motion and further injury.
Sprain
Tear of a ligament (bone-to-bone connection) from excessive joint motion.
Strain
Tear of a muscle or tendon from overuse or trauma; less serious than sprain typically.
Traction Splint
A specialized splint that applies gentle longitudinal traction to stabilize femur fractures, reducing pain and internal bleeding.
The 6 Ps
Pain, Pallor, Pulse, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Pressure — a systematic approach to assessing neurovascular status distal to injury.
